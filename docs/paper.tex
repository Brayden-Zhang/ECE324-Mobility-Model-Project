\begin{abstract}
Trajectory foundation models learn general-purpose mobility representations from large GPS corpora, but they are usually trained with token-level objectives that implicitly overweight longer trajectories. This can hide systematic performance disparities across sequence length. We present a rigorous study of \textbf{TrajectoryFM-HMT}, a hierarchical mobility transformer with multi-resolution \textbf{H3} tokens, flow matching, destination uncertainty modeling, and multi-scale consistency objectives. We introduce three key contributions: (i) \textbf{Length-Balanced Token Loss} (LBTL), (ii) \textbf{Length Sensitivity Gap} (LSG), and (iii) \textbf{LengthRobustBench}, a consolidated robustness protocol. On WorldTrace-derived evaluations, we observe negative LSG for both reconstruction and destination tasks, strong clean performance against an external UniTraj baseline under a shared wrapper, and clear robustness degradation under severe perturbations, and we propose concrete scaling directions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large-scale mobility datasets have enabled trajectory foundation models for reconstruction, next-step prediction, destination inference, and representation transfer \citep{choudhury2024trajectory,zhu2025unitraj}. Yet sequence length remains an under-characterized confounder: mobility traces naturally span very short local trips to long commutes, while most objectives average over tokens rather than trajectories.

This induces a mismatch between training mass and deployment behavior. Long trajectories contribute more supervised positions, while endpoint tasks over long horizons accumulate uncertainty. Aggregate metrics can therefore mask systematic short-vs-long performance gaps.

In this paper we reframe length as a first-class axis of evaluation and architecture analysis. We thoroughly evaluate and establish these claims across our comprehensive experimental suite.

Through this work, we aim to answer four questions: whether trajectory models show systematic long-minus-short degradation; whether length-balanced training reduces this gap; whether the gap remains under invariance and temporal-order stress tests; and how strong clean performance relates to robustness under perturbations.

\paragraph{Contributions}
We present an evaluation-centric study of length sensitivity using TrajectoryFM-HMT as a controlled testbed. We propose \textbf{Length-Balanced Token Loss} (LBTL) as a length-aware training objective, define the \textbf{Length Sensitivity Gap} (LSG), and introduce \textbf{LengthRobustBench}, a unified protocol spanning length stratification, robustness checks, and meter-scale prediction. \section{Related Work}
\paragraph{Trajectory representation and foundation modeling.}
Trajectory modeling has evolved from next-location forecasting architectures such as DeepMove \citep{feng2018deepmove} to large-scale pretraining paradigms designed to learn reusable mobility representations across tasks and regions \citep{zhang2023regions,lin2024trajfm,ma2024jgrm,choudhury2024trajectory,zhu2025unitraj}. This progression mirrors broader foundation-model trends: larger corpora, multi-objective training, and richer transfer evaluations. Our work operates in this regime but focuses on an under-reported axis, whether performance is stable across trajectory length rather than only strong on aggregate metrics.

\paragraph{Generative and uncertainty-aware trajectory learning.}
Recent trajectory research has also emphasized generative formulations and stochastic forecasting, including diffusion-based synthesis \citep{zhu2023difftraj} and contrastive objectives for prediction quality \citep{yao2024trajclip}. We share the goal of robust mobility modeling, but center evaluation on length-conditioned behavior in reconstruction and destination tasks, and on protocol-level robustness under controlled perturbations.

\paragraph{Length sensitivity in long-context sequence models.}
Length effects are well studied in general sequence modeling: Transformer baselines \citep{vaswani2017attention}, long-context attention designs \citep{beltagy2020longformer,zaheer2020bigbird}, positional/extrapolation strategies \citep{su2021roformer,press2022trainshort}, and context-usage diagnostics \citep{liu2024lost}. These advances motivate our analysis, but they do not directly provide mobility-specific diagnostics for short-vs-long degradation under trajectory perturbations.

\paragraph{Positioning of this paper.}
Relative to prior work, our contributions target the gap between strong average performance and length-robust behavior: LBTL rebalances token loss at trajectory granularity, LSG directly quantifies long-minus-short gaps, and LengthRobustBench unifies length stratification with invariance, order-stress, embedding-stability, and meter-scale recovery/prediction tests.

\section{Problem Formulation: Length Bias in Trajectory Models}
We first formalize why standard training objectives systematically under-supervise long trajectories and how error accumulation creates a robust performance gap.

\subsection{Mechanisms of Degradation}
We identify four coupled mechanisms that degrade performance for long trajectories:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Objective-Mass Imbalance}: Token-level averaging provides longer sequences with disproportionate gradient mass, but per-trajectory supervision is diluted.
    \item \textbf{Compounding Endpoint Uncertainty}: Destination prediction integrates local errors over extended horizons, causing variance to grow with length $L$.
    \item \textbf{Hierarchical Compression Noise}: Aggregating step-level tokens into regions introduces information loss that scales with trajectory complexity.
    \item \textbf{Context Dilution}: Within a fixed model capacity, expanding contexts linearly amplifies the number of competing tokens, challenging long-range signal preservation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Length-Balanced Token Loss (LBTL)}
To address length bias, we introduce LBTL. Standard masked cross-entropy is effectively length-weighted:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{\text{std}}_{\text{tok}}=\frac{\sum_b\sum_{t=1}^{m_b}\ell_{b,t}}{\sum_b m_b}
\end{equation}
LBTL strictly enforces equal mass per trajectory, regardless of length $m_b$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{\text{lb}}_{\text{tok}}=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\left(\frac{1}{\max(1,m_b)}\sum_{t=1}^{m_b}\ell_{b,t}\right)
\end{equation}
This normalization ensures that short, local trips contribute equally to gradient updates as long commutes, stabilizing optimization across the length spectrum.
Total training loss combines reconstruction, flow matching, and destination probing:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = w_{\text{tok}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{lb}} + w_{\text{flow}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{flow}} + w_{\text{dest}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{dest}} + w_{\text{misc}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{misc}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Preprocessing pipeline}
We process raw GPS trajectories $\mathcal{T} = \{\mathbf{p}_1, \dots, \mathbf{p}_N\}$, where each point $\mathbf{p}_i$ is a tuple of latitude, longitude, and timestamp. We normalize all coordinates to the range $[-90^{\circ}, 90^{\circ}] \times [-180^{\circ}, 180^{\circ}]$.

Trajectories longer than our context window $L_{\max}$ are downsampled to fit. The model inputs are: (1) spatial coordinates $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times L_{\max} \times 2}$, (2) timestamps $\mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times L_{\max}}$, and (3) boolean attention masks $\mathbf{M}$. We use a dynamic masking strategy during training, where the masking probability $p_{\mathrm{mask}}$ is annealed over time.


\subsection{Evaluation Protocol: LengthRobustBench}

To carefully measure how trajectory models perform across different sequence lengths and input perturbations, we introduce \textbf{LengthRobustBench}, an end-to-end testing suite. 

\subsubsection{Length Sensitivity Gap (LSG)}
We define the Length Sensitivity Gap (LSG) to quantify performance differences across trajectory lengths. We partition the test set into subsets based on length quantiles: short ($\mathcal{S}_{\text{short}}$) and long ($\mathcal{S}_{\text{long}}$). For a given metric $\mathcal{M}$ (where higher is better), the mean performance on a subset $\mathcal{S}$ is $\mu_{\mathcal{M}}(\mathcal{S})$.

We define LSG as the difference in performance between long and short trajectories:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{LSG}_{\mathcal{M}} = \mu_{\mathcal{M}}(\mathcal{S}_{\text{long}}) - \mu_{\mathcal{M}}(\mathcal{S}_{\text{short}})
\end{equation}
A negative LSG indicates that the model performs worse on longer trajectories, revealing length bias. A value close to zero indicates length robustness.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
We use the following metrics to evaluate model performance:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Recon@L1}: The mean absolute error ($\ell_1$ distance in meters) between predicted and actual coordinates for masked steps.
    \item \textbf{Next@Top1}: Adjusted accuracy for predicting the immediate next H3 token in the sequence.
    \item \textbf{Dest-Local@Top1}: Top-1 accuracy for destination prediction, evaluated within a dynamic local candidate set (radius constrained).
    \item \textbf{Dest-Global@Top1}: Top-1 accuracy for destination prediction over the entire global vocabulary.
    \item \textbf{Change Detection AUC}: Area Under the ROC Curve for distinguishing between valid trajectory continuations and anomalous/perturbed ones.
    \item \textbf{NLL}: Negative Log-Likelihood of the ground truth sequence, measuring model uncertainty.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training and Evaluation Details}
\paragraph{Data regime and stratification.}
Unless noted otherwise, experiments use a fixed 2,000-trajectory subset. Length analysis uses the quantile thresholds in Table~\ref{tab:length} (164 and 601), yielding short/medium/long strata for LSG computation.

\paragraph{Model and checkpoint protocol.}
Primary results uniformly evaluate our designated principal checkpoint. Ablations share the same wrapper and reporting format so differences can be interpreted as controlled objective/component changes rather than changes in metric code.

\paragraph{Metric protocol.}
We report Recon@L1, Next@Top1, and two destination metrics with different label spaces: Dest-Global@Top1 (strict full-vocabulary destination classification) and Dest-Local@Top1 (candidate-constrained destination ranking in LengthRobustBench).

\paragraph{Open reproducibility package.}
The repository includes the paper source, checked-in summary artifacts, and code paths that regenerate the paper figures and tables from those artifacts. Large checkpoints and raw cache directories are intentionally kept outside Git.

\paragraph{Reporting scope.}
Main tables report deterministic point estimates from fixed checkpoints and dataset splits. Where the checked-in repository preserves only summary-level artifacts, we report those values directly and avoid unsupported claims about multi-run training variance.

\section{Scaling and Future Work}
Future scaling directions are straightforward from existing infrastructure: (i) full-scale multi-epoch WorldTrace with geographic OOD splits; (ii) longer-context sparse attention and hierarchical memory; (iii) dynamic multi-objective reweighting and robustness curricula; and (iv) strict robust benchmark parity runs against UniTraj under matched perturbation budgets and compute.

\section{Discussion}
Overall, the current evidence shows that TrajectoryFM-HMT is strong in clean meter-scale recovery and prediction, but still exhibits a measurable long-horizon destination gap and a clear reverse-order failure mode. The main contribution is therefore not a blanket robustness claim; it is a reproducible length-sensitivity evaluation framing with concrete, code-backed diagnostics.

\section{Limitations and Ethics}
Our analysis emphasizes length, but mobility models should also be audited for geographic, socioeconomic, and temporal subgroup bias. Some repo extensions are currently validated on synthetic or proxy settings and require large real-world studies before deployment conclusions. As always with trajectory data, privacy risk remains material even after anonymization; deployment should include strict governance, aggregation controls, and privacy-preserving training where feasible.

\section{Broader Impacts and Safeguards}
\paragraph{Potential positive impact.}
Length-robust trajectory modeling can improve transportation planning, demand forecasting, and emergency response routing by reducing short-vs-long trip performance disparity and improving reliability under perturbations.

\paragraph{Potential negative impact.}
Mobility models can be misused for surveillance, sensitive population monitoring, or unfair allocation decisions if deployed without governance. Even anonymized trajectories can carry re-identification risk when combined with auxiliary data.

\paragraph{Safeguards in this release.}
We provide a reproducibility-oriented research release (code, scripts, checked-in summary artifacts, and figure/table generation paths) and do not present deployment claims. We explicitly document robustness failure modes, encourage subgroup auditing before real-world use, and recommend access controls, aggregation thresholds, and privacy-preserving training for operational settings.

\section{Conclusion}
This work delivers an evaluation-first analysis of length sensitivity in trajectory foundation modeling. Using TrajectoryFM-HMT as an instrumented backbone, we introduce LBTL, LSG, and LengthRobustBench, and provide reproducible evidence that length materially affects behavior even when aggregate performance is strong.
